# Guardrails — Market research

Mistakes founders make systematically when researching markets and validating hypotheses. Read this before running any interviews.

---

## Defining the hypothesis

❌ Starting to collect data without a clear hypothesis
✅ Define first: "I want to test whether X is true for segment Y in context Z"

❌ Hypothesis too broad ("is there demand for this?")
✅ Falsifiable hypothesis: "SMBs in retail with 5–50 employees spend over $2k/month on X and have no satisfying solution"

---

## Selecting interviewees

❌ Interviewing only accessible people — friends, ex-colleagues, your network
✅ Recruit outside your bubble; include people who did NOT buy and who use the alternative solution

❌ Interviewing only enthusiastic early adopters
✅ Include the "mainstream" customer — the majority of the market is not an enthusiast

❌ Too few interviews ("I did 5 and it's already clear")
✅ Continue until pattern saturation — usually 15–25 interviews per segment

---

## Conducting interviews

❌ Asking "would you use this product?"
✅ Ask about past behavior: "how did you solve this the last time it happened?"

❌ Presenting the solution before understanding the problem
✅ Spend 80% of the interview on the problem. The solution comes at the end, only if necessary

❌ Asking closed questions ("is this a problem for you?")
✅ Ask open questions ("tell me how you handle X today")

❌ Filling silence with suggestions ("did you mean that...")
✅ Let the interviewee finish their thought — the insights are in the pauses

❌ Accepting "yes, I'd be interested" as validation
✅ Real validation = behavior: payment, letter of intent, time dedicated, sensitive data shared

---

## Interpreting data

❌ Focusing on what people say they want
✅ Focusing on what people do — real behavior, not declared intention

❌ Confirming hypothesis with a relative majority ("7 out of 10 found it interesting")
✅ Look for signals of acute pain in a committed minority ("3 people said it's critical and already pay for a workaround")

❌ Ignoring responses that don't fit the hypothesis
✅ Exceptions are data — what makes this profile different?

❌ Aggregating responses from different segments ("customers think that...")
✅ Segment before aggregating — different patterns in different segments is the most valuable finding

---

## Market sizing

❌ Using top-down TAM without bottom-up validation ("the SaaS market in Brazil is worth $10bn")
✅ Triangulate: top-down + bottom-up + analogy with a similar market in another country

❌ Reporting TAM as if it were your addressable market
✅ Use SAM (segment you can serve today) and SOM (what you can capture in 3 years). See [[tam-sam-som]]

❌ Estimating TAM without questioning the data source
✅ Ask: who produced this number, when, and with what methodology?

---

## Classic pitfalls

❌ **Mom test failure** — anyone who likes you won't tell you your idea is bad
✅ Seek people who have the problem, not people who like you

❌ **False validation through enthusiasm** — "loved the idea!" is not data
✅ Enthusiasm without commitment (time, money, data) is noise

❌ **Confirmation bias in analysis** — selecting quotes that confirm the hypothesis
✅ Document all responses, including those that contradict. See [[cognitive-biases-founders]]

❌ **Confusing problem with solution** — interviewing about your solution instead of the problem
✅ The goal of research is to deeply understand the problem, not validate your solution
