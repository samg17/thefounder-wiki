# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Framework for understanding what the customer is really "hiring" your product to do. The unit of analysis is not the product nor the customer — it's the **job** that needs to be done.

**Origin:** Developed by Clayton Christensen. The central phrase: *"People don't want a quarter-inch drill — they want a quarter-inch hole."*

**When to use:** When defining ICP, when designing features, when writing product copy, when understanding why customers churned.

---

## The core concept

Customers don't buy products — they **hire** products to do a job when a specific circumstance arises.

The job has three dimensions:
- **Functional:** what needs to be done objectively ("I need to get to the airport by 6am")
- **Emotional:** how the customer wants to feel ("without anxiety, without rushing")
- **Social:** how the customer wants to be seen by others ("professional, organized")

A product that only solves the functional dimension is commoditizable. Products that solve all three dimensions have greater differentiation and lower price elasticity.

---

## Functional vs. emotional vs. social jobs

| Type | Key question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Functional | What needs to be done? | Send a report to the client |
| Emotional | How does the customer want to feel? | Confident, without fear of error |
| Social | How does the customer want to be seen? | As a competent, organized professional |

**Common mistake:** Focusing only on the functional job and ignoring the other two. The emotional job is often the most important and the least articulated by the customer.

## How to identify jobs

### Job-oriented interviews

Useful questions:
- "Tell me about the last time you [did X]. What happened?"
- "What made you look for a solution at that moment?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish when you decided to [action]?"
- "What did you do before using [current product]? Why did you switch?"
- "When you don't use [product], what do you use instead?"

**Avoid:** "What do you want the product to do?" — customers describe features, not jobs.

### Job map

For each job identified, document:
1. **Circumstance:** when/where the job arises
2. **Motivation:** what the customer is trying to accomplish
3. **Obstacle:** what makes it difficult or impossible today
4. **Current alternatives:** what the customer uses today (even if imperfect)
5. **Success criteria:** how the customer knows the job was done well

---

## Competition through JTBD

The framework redefines competition: **every product the customer can hire for the same job is a competitor**, even if it's in a different category.

Examples:
- Fast food milkshake competes with banana and bagel (job: easy breakfast in the car)
- Notion competes with paper and email, not just other note apps
- Uber competes with owning a car, not just with taxis

**For founders:** Ask "what would the customer do if our product didn't exist?" The answer is your real competitor list.

---

## JTBD in product practice

### Feature prioritization
Before building a feature, identify which job it solves. Features without a clear job = product debt.

### Marketing copy
Communicate the job and the circumstance, not just the features. "For when you need to present complex data to a time-pressed client" is more effective than listing functionalities.

### Retention and churn
When a customer churns, they "fired" your product. Job-oriented churn interviews reveal: which job the product stopped solving, or which alternative now solves it better.

---

## Limitations

- Jobs are hard to articulate for entertainment and discovery products (the job is emergent)
- Doesn't capture habit and addiction well — the behavior isn't oriented toward a conscious "job"
- In complex B2B markets, the end user's job may differ from the buyer's job — map both
