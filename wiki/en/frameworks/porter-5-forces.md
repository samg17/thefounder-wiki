# Porter — 5 forces

Framework for evaluating the structural attractiveness of a market and its competitive dynamics. Use when entering a new market or reviewing your position.

**When to use:** Before entering a market, when raising capital (investors will ask), when reviewing strategy annually.

**When not to use:** Does not replace customer research. Does not predict the future. Markets undergoing technological disruption have dynamics the framework doesn't capture well.

---

## The 5 forces

### 1. Rivalry among existing competitors

What determines intensity:
- Number of players and market concentration
- Market growth rate (stagnant markets = price wars)
- Differentiation between products
- Exit barriers (specific assets, contracts, emotional cost)

**High rivalry** → compressed margins, price wars, hard to build a moat
**Low rivalry** → room for healthy margins and positioning

**For founders:** High rivalry doesn't mean you shouldn't enter — it means you need clear differentiation or an angle existing players can't or won't occupy.

---

### 2. Threat of new entrants

What determines the barrier:
- Economies of scale (incumbents have lower cost per unit)
- Access to distribution channels
- Capital requirements
- Cost advantages independent of scale (location, patents, learning curve)
- Regulation and licenses
- Customer switching costs (how much it costs the customer to switch suppliers)

**High threat** → margins tend to be low, attractive markets invite entrants
**Low threat** → incumbents protected, harder to challenge but better margins

**For founders:** You ARE the new entrant. Identify which barriers exist and whether you can bypass them with a different model (e.g. going direct to consumer instead of through the traditional channel).

---

### 3. Bargaining power of suppliers

What increases supplier power:
- Few alternative suppliers
- Supplier's product is critical and hard to substitute
- High cost to switch suppliers
- Supplier can integrate vertically and compete with you

**High power** → supplier compresses your margin, you become dependent
**Low power** → you have options, can negotiate price and terms

**For founders:** Map your critical dependencies. AWS, Stripe, a single component supplier — all are concentration risks. Which supplier, if they raised prices 50%, would break your model?

---

### 4. Bargaining power of buyers

What increases buyer power:
- Few buyers or very concentrated buyers (e.g. 3 customers = 80% of revenue)
- Undifferentiated product (easy to switch suppliers)
- Low switching cost for the buyer
- Buyer can integrate vertically and do it themselves

**High power** → customers dictate price and terms, compressed margins
**Low power** → you have pricing power, can maintain margins

**For founders:** If 1–2 customers represent more than 30% of your revenue, you have concentration risk. They have disproportionate power over you.

---

### 5. Threat of substitute products

Substitutes are products from **other markets** that satisfy the same customer job.

Examples:
- Video conferencing substituted business travel (different markets, same job)
- Streaming substituted video rental (different markets, same job)
- WhatsApp substituted SMS

What determines the threat:
- Price-performance of the substitute vs your product
- Migration cost to the substitute
- Buyer's propensity to substitute

**For founders:** The most dangerous substitute is often not a direct competitor — it's a completely different way of solving the same problem. See [[jobs-to-be-done]].

---

## How to use the framework

**Step 1:** For each force, assess: High / Medium / Low and why (be specific)

**Step 2:** Identify the 1–2 forces most determinant for your market

**Step 3:** Determine whether the market structure favors above-average returns

**Step 4:** Identify how your positioning can mitigate unfavorable forces

---

## Limitations

- Static framework — markets change, especially with technological disruption
- Doesn't capture platform markets and network effects well (different dynamics)
- Qualitative analysis — results depend on the quality of assumptions
- Does not replace real market and customer data

⚠️ Potential contradiction: Porter assumes a relatively stable market structure. In emerging markets (where many startups operate), the forces are still being defined — apply with caution.
