# ICP — Ideal Customer Profile

Definition of the customer profile that benefits most from your product, is most likely to buy, and generates the most value for the business.

**Why it matters:** Without a clear ICP, sales wastes time on wrong leads, marketing shoots in every direction, and the product becomes too generic to be excellent for anyone.

---

## ICP vs. Persona

| | ICP | Persona |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Company/organization (B2B) or segment (B2C) | Specific individual |
| Use | Lead qualification, go-to-market strategy | Communication, UX, content |
| Level | Strategic | Tactical |

In B2B, you need both: the ICP defines the **target company**, the persona defines **who inside the company** you need to reach and convince.

---

## What defines a good ICP

### Firmographic dimensions (B2B)

- **Segment/vertical:** which industry or type of business
- **Size:** number of employees, annual revenue
- **Stage:** startup, SMB, mid-market, enterprise
- **Business model:** SaaS, service, physical product, marketplace
- **Location:** geographic, language, regulation
- **Technology maturity:** uses similar tools? Has a technical team?

### Behavioral dimensions

- **Problem frequency:** how often does the job arise?
- **Pain intensity:** how much does the problem cost (time, money, risk)?
- **Urgency:** does the problem need to be solved now or can it wait?
- **Budget:** is there budget available or does it need to be created?
- **Buying process:** who decides, who influences, how long does it take?

### Fit dimensions

- **Willingness to pay:** does the ICP see enough value to pay your price?
- **Cost to serve:** how much does it cost to deliver value to this profile?
- **Expansion potential:** does the customer tend to expand usage over time?
- **NPS/referrals:** does this profile recommend to others?

---

## How to define your ICP

### Step 1: Analyze your best current customers

If you already have customers, start with the 20% that:
- Have the lowest churn / highest retention
- Generate the highest LTV
- Have the lowest CAC
- Give the most positive feedback and referrals

What do these customers have in common? That intersection is the starting point of the ICP.

### Step 2: Interview the best (and the worst)

Questions for the best:
- What made them choose your product?
- What concrete impact has the product had?
- What would happen if they stopped using it?

Questions for those who churn or are difficult:
- What didn't work?
- What did they expect that didn't happen?

The contrast between the two profiles reveals the ICP.

### Step 3: Test segmentation hypotheses

Formulate specific, falsifiable hypotheses:

> "Professional services companies (accounting, legal) with 10–50 employees have 40% lower CAC and 2x higher LTV than the average customer because [reason]."

Validate with cohort data. If the hypothesis doesn't hold in the data, adjust.

### Step 4: Document and distribute

The ICP needs to be:
- Specific enough to qualify leads in 5 minutes
- Shared with sales, marketing and product
- Reviewed every 6–12 months as the product and market evolve

---

## Signs of a wrong or undefined ICP

- Full pipeline but low conversion rate
- Lots of churn in the first 90 days
- Customers request very different features from each other
- Sales is slow because you need to educate the market a lot
- Low or neutral NPS — nobody loves the product

---

## Pitfalls

❌ ICP too broad ("mid-sized companies in Brazil")
✅ Specific enough to exclude: "service companies with 10–50 employees that already use some CRM but still manage proposals in spreadsheets"

❌ Defining ICP by intuition without real customer data
✅ Derive ICP from cohort analysis and interviews with best and worst customers

❌ Fixed ICP that is never revised
✅ Revisit as the product evolves and new segments emerge

❌ Different ICP for each person on the team
✅ Shared document, validated, with objective qualification criteria
