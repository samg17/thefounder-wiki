# Cognitive biases — founders

Systematic judgment distortions that affect founders in business decisions. Knowing them doesn't eliminate them, but allows you to build a verification checklist.

---

## Validation biases

### Confirmation bias
Tendency to seek, interpret and remember information that confirms existing beliefs.

**How it shows up:** Selecting interview quotes that validate the hypothesis and ignoring those that contradict. Giving more weight to positive investor feedback than to silent customer churn.

**Antidote:** Document all responses, including negative ones. Ask someone without bias to review the raw data. Actively look for evidence that falsifies your hypothesis.

---

### Anchoring bias
Tendency to give excessive weight to the first piece of information received.

**How it shows up:** The first TAM number you read becomes the reference for everything. The first customer who paid sets the price forever. The first valuation offer anchors all subsequent negotiations.

**Antidote:** Generate multiple independent estimates before looking at external data. Explicitly question the anchor: "what if this number were wrong by a factor of 10?"

---

### Availability bias
Tendency to overestimate the probability of events that are easy to recall or imagine.

**How it shows up:** Overestimating the risk from a specific competitor because you just read about them. Underestimating execution risks because they've never happened to you.

**Antidote:** Base rates. Before estimating the probability of any event, ask: "what is the historical frequency of this type of event in similar companies?"

---

## Optimism biases

### Overconfidence
Tendency to overestimate the precision of your own predictions.

**How it shows up:** Growth projections that assume everything goes right. Deadlines with no buffer. CAC estimates that don't account for channel saturation.

**Antidote:** Build explicit pessimistic scenarios. Ask: "if I'm wrong, how would I be wrong?" Ask someone outside to make the projection independently.

---

### Planning fallacy
Tendency to underestimate the time, cost and risks of future actions.

**How it shows up:** "We launch in 3 months" becomes 9. "It'll cost $200k" becomes $600k. Every project takes longer and costs more than planned.

**Antidote:** Use base rates from similar projects. Multiply time estimates by 1.5–2x. Set aside a 20–30% contingency budget.

---

### Optimism bias
Tendency to believe you're less likely to experience negative events than others.

**How it shows up:** "Most startups fail, but ours is different because..." Ignoring execution risks that affected similar companies.

**Antidote:** Pre-mortem: imagine it's one year from now and the company failed. What went wrong? List the reasons.

---

## Persistence biases

### Sunk cost fallacy
Tendency to keep investing in something because of what's already been invested, not because of future return.

**How it shows up:** Continuing to develop a feature because "we already spent 3 months on it." Keeping a problematic co-founder because "we built everything together." Not pivoting because "we already communicated this direction to investors."

**Antidote:** Ignore the past in the decision. Ask: "if I were starting today from scratch, with what I know now, would I make this decision?" If not, sunk cost is holding you back.

---

### Status quo bias
Tendency to prefer the current state of things and perceive change as loss.

**How it shows up:** Resisting pivoting even with clear evidence. Maintaining inefficient processes because "it's always been done this way." Not questioning founding hypotheses even after contradictory data.

**Antidote:** Treat the status quo as one option among many, not as the default. Ask: "if we weren't doing X today, would we decide to start doing it?"

---

## Social biases

### Groupthink
Tendency of groups to converge toward consensus and suppress dissenting opinions.

**How it shows up:** Founder team aligns quickly without real questioning. Meetings where nobody disagrees with the CEO. Decisions that "everyone agrees on" but nobody individually committed to.

**Antidote:** Explicitly designate a "devil's advocate" in important decisions. Collect opinions individually before group discussion. The CEO speaks last.

---

### Authority bias
Tendency to give excessive weight to the opinion of authority figures.

**How it shows up:** Changing strategy because a famous investor commented something. Adopting a framework because a successful founder recommended it. Ignoring your own data because a consultant said otherwise.

**Antidote:** Evaluate the argument, not who made it. Ask: "what data supports this claim?" The authority may be right for the wrong reason — or wrong.

---

## Decision checklist

Before important decisions, ask:

1. Am I seeking evidence that **contradicts** my hypothesis, or only those that confirm it?
2. What is the historical **base rate** of this type of outcome in similar companies?
3. Am I continuing because of **future return** or because of what I've already invested?
4. If I were starting **today from scratch**, would I make this decision?
5. What would have to be true for me to be **completely wrong**?
